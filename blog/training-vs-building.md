# Training vs. Building: Why Both Matter

**Published**: May 2026 | **Read time**: 6 min

There's an ongoing debate in the AI community: Should you focus on building production systems or training others? The answer is both matter—they're complementary, not competitive.

---

## The Training Perspective

**Advocates say:**
- "Education multiplies impact exponentially"
- "Teaching prevents repeated mistakes"
- "Knowledge transfer is valuable"
- "Training creates a stronger ecosystem"

**They're right about:**
- ✅ One trained person can influence many
- ✅ Shared knowledge prevents duplication
- ✅ Education raises ecosystem maturity
- ✅ Training creates networks and communities

**But training alone struggles with:**
- ❌ Accountability for outcomes
- ❌ Limited scalability of direct impact
- ❌ Students may not implement what they learn
- ❌ Knowledge becomes stale without practice

---

## The Building Perspective

**Advocates say:**
- "Real impact comes from shipped products"
- "Building teaches more than talking"
- "Systems can scale to millions"
- "Business value justifies investment"

**They're right about:**
- ✅ Products directly serve users
- ✅ Building generates real learning
- ✅ Systems scale without additional effort
- ✅ Measurable business impact

**But building alone struggles with:**
- ❌ Limited knowledge transfer
- ❌ Context-specific solutions don't generalize
- ❌ Doesn't help the broader ecosystem
- ❌ Single points of failure (one company, one system)

---

## Why They Need Each Other

### Problem: Skills Gap
There aren't enough AI engineers. Training can help.

But training without real systems to reference is abstract.

**Solution**: Builders share patterns. Trainers contextualize them.

### Problem: Implementation Gap
Many organizations understand AI but struggle to implement.

Training helps with the understanding part. But applying that to real systems is different.

**Solution**: Builders create case studies. Trainers use them as teaching material.

### Problem: Innovation Gap
Building in isolation means missing knowledge from the field.

Training without feedback from builders means teaching becomes outdated.

**Solution**: Builders and trainers maintain dialogue. One informs the other.

### Problem: Speed Gap
The AI field moves fast. Training can't keep up if it's disconnected from practice.

Building alone doesn't scale knowledge fast enough.

**Solution**: Close feedback loops. Builders inform trainers who spread knowledge.

---

## The Complementary Value

### Training Creates Foundation

**Trainers provide:**
- Conceptual understanding
- Best practices patterns
- Implementation strategies
- Ecosystem knowledge

**For builders, this means:**
- Better hiring (more educated candidates)
- Shared language and standards
- Faster adoption of systems
- Quality implementations

### Building Creates Proof Points

**Builders provide:**
- Real-world case studies
- Concrete implementation patterns
- Honest failure stories
- Measured outcomes

**For trainers, this means:**
- Current material to teach
- Real examples vs. theory
- Credibility with students
- Evidence of what works

---

## The Ideal Model

### For Individuals

**Early Career**: Focus on learning. Take training, build small projects, understand patterns.

**Growth Phase**: Balance teaching and building. Start a project, mentor juniors, share learnings.

**Mature Phase**: Build your main systems, mentor teams, contribute to ecosystem knowledge.

**Later Career**: Strategic advice, ecosystem building, policy and education influence.

### For Organizations

**Best companies do both:**
- Google: Builds products AND publishes research AND runs training
- DeepMind: Builds AI systems AND publishes findings AND mentors talent
- OpenAI: Builds systems AND publishes papers AND engages policy

They understand the multiplier effect of combining building and sharing.

### For the Ecosystem

**Healthy ecosystems have:**
- 70% focused on building (creating actual value)
- 20% focused on teaching (sharing knowledge)
- 10% focused on research (pushing frontiers)

Too much teaching, not enough building → theory without practice
Too much building, no teaching → siloed knowledge, repeated mistakes

## For You

Whichever you choose, recognize:

**If you're building**: Share what you learn. Write about it. Mentor others. Your systems will be more robust, and you'll influence the field beyond your code.

**If you're training**: Partner with builders. Use real examples. Keep learning yourself. Your teaching will be more credible and impactful.

**If you're early in your journey**: Do both. Train yourself by teaching. Build to learn. The diversity of experience makes you better at both.

---

**The false dichotomy is the real problem. The real question isn't "training or building?" It's "how do we create ecosystems where both thrive?"**
